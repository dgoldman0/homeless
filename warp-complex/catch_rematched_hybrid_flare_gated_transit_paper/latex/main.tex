\begin{abstract}
This paper presents a standalone reduced-engineering reference model for a composite faster-than-light spacetime architecture under the source-available assumption. The model combines hybrid flare-gated wormhole infrastructure with a catch-rematched throat-loaded transit packet. The core result is a design architecture: passenger safety, infrastructure accessibility, transition-current burden, and spatial-curvature burden become separate engineering channels. The frozen v1 candidate treats the throat as active rail infrastructure and the passenger as a protected moving packet. The packet is caught before shift fade, shift support fades before throat relaxation, and the shift remains contained inside the same throat support envelope that supplies lapse and capacity. The frozen balanced default uses
\[
C_0=20,\quad C_\perp=5,\quad R_{\rm th}=1.25,\quad w_{\rm th}=0.12,\quad R_{\rm pass}=0.35,\quad w_{\rm pass}=0.08,\quad B_0=6,\quad w_B=12,
\]
with
\[
x_{\rm catch}=0.05,\quad x_\beta=0.70,\quad x_q=1.25,\quad w_{\rm catch}=0.25,\quad w_\beta=0.28,\quad w_q=0.30,\quad p_\beta=4.
\]
The reduced ADM screens show that transition profiles govern the dynamic/current channel $(j_M,K)$, while capacity/support geometry governs the Hamiltonian/spatial-curvature channel $(\rho_H,{}^{(3)}R)$. The three-scenario geometry confirmation reduces total source-demand proxy to about $0.162\times$ the v01 geometry baseline, geometry cost to $0.274\times$, and dynamic cost to $0.0108\times$. The conservative current-floor source screen reduces Lorentzian-sampled packet negative exposure at $V=10$, $\lambda=6$, and $\tau=0.2$ to about $0.042\times$ baseline. Robustness checks identify a mixed-capacity operating band, with $C_\perp=5$ as the balanced default and $C_\perp=3$ as a pressure/current-leaning branch. The paper's central contribution is a burden-distribution map for source-supplied spacetime transport: the passenger rides a packet, the throat acts as plant, transition profiles manage current, and angular capacity manages spatial-curvature concentration.
\end{abstract}

\section{The engineering object}

The usual language of traversable wormholes and warp drives begins with a shape. A throat connects two regions. A bubble moves through an exterior. A route is prepared. These shapes are the right starting point for geometry. A transport system needs one more layer of description. It needs a service object.

A passenger system is a sequence of controlled events. Support is prepared. Capacity is supplied. Motion is coupled. The passenger is caught. The transport field is faded. The infrastructure relaxes and resets. The engineering object is the whole service process together with the metric at each instant.

The combined architecture developed here takes that service view seriously. The wormhole throat is active infrastructure. It stretches, opens, gates, catches, supports, and relaxes. The passenger is represented by a finite packet. The packet is the cabin-level object; the throat is the plant. The transport field is the controlled coupling between the two.

This framing changes the technical question. A passive tunnel design asks for a broadly comfortable throat. A standalone bubble design asks the moving wall to carry both transport and passenger protection. The composite design asks how burden should be distributed across a source-supplied plant:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\hbox{packet safety}+\hbox{transition current control}+\hbox{capacity support}+\hbox{spatial-curvature management}+\hbox{infrastructure reset}.}
\label{eq:service_object}
\end{equation}
The frozen v1 candidate is the first reduced model in this line that makes that distribution explicit.

\section{The contribution in engineering terms}

The paper contributes four concrete design results.

First, it separates passenger safety from infrastructure accessibility. The passenger criterion is the moving packet worldtube. Stationary $g_{tt}$ remains an infrastructure and station-access diagnostic. This separation lets the model evaluate what the passenger actually rides while treating passive comfort throughout the active plant as an infrastructure question.

Second, it identifies catch/rematch choreography as the hard packet gate. The service order
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\hbox{catch}\rightarrow\hbox{shift fade}\rightarrow\hbox{throat relaxation}}
\label{eq:catch_fade_relax}
\end{equation}
keeps the packet supported through the shift transition. Late catch produces packet failure because the still-fast packet loses the support field that carried it.

Third, it separates burden channels. Transition profiles move $j_M$ and $K$. Capacity/support geometry moves $\rho_H$ and ${}^{(3)}R$. This turns a broad exotic-source burden into two engineering subsystems.

Fourth, it identifies mixed radial/angular capacity as the spatial-burden solution in the tested composite family. The angular capacity $C_\perp$ is a curvature-management control. The useful range is mixed-capacity, with $C_\perp=5$ as the balanced default and $C_\perp=3$ as a pressure/current-leaning branch.

Together these results turn the combined wormhole/warp construction into a source-supplied service architecture. The literature supplies the physical warning signs: exotic support, thin-wall severity, length-scale pressure, capacity bookkeeping, prepared infrastructure, and ADM role separation. The present result shows how those pressures distribute when the system is engineered as active throat infrastructure carrying a protected packet.

\section{What the literature makes clear, and what this model adds}

The field already provides the boundary conditions for any serious FTL geometry. Morris and Thorne establish the traversable throat as a geometric object with flare-out, finite redshift, low tidal-force, and exotic-support requirements \citep{morris_thorne_1988}. Morris, Thorne, and Yurtsever connect wormhole mouth arrangements to chronology control \citep{morris_thorne_yurtsever_1988}. Ford and Roman show that quantum inequalities turn negative energy into a magnitude-duration and length-scale problem \citep{ford_roman_1996}; Fewster and Roman sharpen the null-contracted sampled-stress pressure for small-exotic-matter proposals \citep{fewster_roman_2005}. Dynamic-throat work keeps local null expansion and local energy-condition diagnostics at the throat \citep{hochberg_visser_1998}.

Warp and prepared-route papers supply complementary pieces. Alcubierre shows how a shift-dominated warp metric can be written in general relativity \citep{alcubierre_1994}. Pfenning and Ford show how the Alcubierre wall concentrates severe quantum-inequality pressure \citep{pfenning_ford_1997}. Van den Broeck shows that spatial capacity can change energy accounting by separating compact exterior size from interior volume \citep{van_den_broeck_1999}. Natario emphasizes the shift-structure view of warp motion beyond expansion/contraction intuition \citep{natario_2002}. Everett and Roman's Krasnikov-tube analysis places prepared infrastructure and causal control at the center of route-based superluminal travel \citep{everett_roman_1997}. Bobrick and Martire provide an ADM language in which lapse, shift, spatial geometry, capacity, and time-rate roles can be separated \citep{bobrick_martire_2021}. Lentz and Fuchs et al. show the continuing value of matter-shell and shift-structure engineering in modern warp-geometry work \citep{lentz_2021,fuchs_2024}. Gao, Jafferis, and Wall show controlled traversability through source timing/coupling in a holographic setting \citep{gao_jafferis_wall_2017}.

Those papers make the stakes legible. They leave an engineering allocation problem. If source support is available, which part of the geometry should carry passenger safety? Which part should carry transition current? Which part should carry spatial curvature? Which field should behave like a rail, which like a cabin, which like a switch, and which like a reset mechanism?

\section{Architecture: the throat becomes plant}

\subsection{Hybrid flare-gated infrastructure}

The throat infrastructure begins with the hybrid flare-gated form
\begin{equation}
 ds^2=-N(l,t)^2\dd t^2+B(l,t)^2\dd l^2+R(l,t)^2\dd\Omega^2.
\label{eq:throat_metric}
\end{equation}
The control roles are direct:
\begin{align}
 B(l,t)&: \hbox{radial proper-length support and service-coordinate stretch},\nonumber\\
 R(l,t)&: \hbox{flare/access state and angular geometry},\nonumber\\
 N(l,t)&: \hbox{timing, lapse margin, and shoulder matching}.\nonumber
\end{align}
The wormhole-only v1 model used these functions to create a phased throat-support plant: prestretch, flare opening, quiet access hold, closure, repayment/shoulder matching, and reset \citep{goldman_hybrid_flare_gated_paper}. The composite model uses the same controls with a new job. They carry the infrastructure around a service packet.

\subsection{Throat-loaded packet}

The packet side is written in radial ADM form,
\begin{equation}
 ds^2=-T^2\dd t^2+A^2(\dd l+\beta^l\dd t)^2+A^2r(l)^2\dd\Omega^2.
\label{eq:packet_basic}
\end{equation}
Capacity and lapse are paired,
\begin{equation}
 A=\exp(qW\ln C_0),\qquad T=\exp(qW\ln(\lambda C_0)),
\label{eq:paired_AT}
\end{equation}
so that
\begin{equation}
 T/A=\lambda^{qW}.
\label{eq:AT_ratio}
\end{equation}
The shift is throat-gated:
\begin{equation}
 \beta^l(l,t)=-U(t)E(X(t))W(l)S(l-X(t)).
\label{eq:throat_gated_shift}
\end{equation}
The support-inclusion rule
\begin{equation}
 \supp(\beta^l)\subseteq\supp(A,T)
\label{eq:support_inclusion}
\end{equation}
keeps the transport shift inside the same region that supplies support. This rule is one of the bridge results from the original catch-rematched transit ansatz \citep{goldman_communication_paper}.

\subsection{Final composite field}

The final composite metric is
\begin{equation}
 ds^2=-\alpha(l,t)^2\dd t^2+
 \gamma_{ll}(l,t)(\dd l+\beta^l(l,t)\dd t)^2+
 \gamma_{\Omega\Omega}(l,t)\dd\Omega^2.
\label{eq:composite_metric}
\end{equation}
The fields are
\begin{align}
 \alpha(l,t)&=N_{\vone}(l,t)T_{\pkt}(l,t),\label{eq:alpha}\\
 \gamma_{ll}(l,t)&=B_{\vone}(l,t)^2A_\parallel(l,t)^2,\label{eq:gll}\\
 \gamma_{\Omega\Omega}(l,t)&=R_{\vone}(l,t)^2A_\perp(l,t)^2,\label{eq:go}\\
 \beta^l(l,t)&=-\dot{X}_{\rm coord}(t)E(X(t))W(l)^{p_\beta}S(l-X(t)).\label{eq:beta_final}
\end{align}
The capacity factors are anisotropic,
\begin{align}
 A_\parallel&=\exp(qW\ln C_0),\label{eq:Apar}\\
 A_\perp&=\exp(qW\ln C_\perp),\label{eq:Aperp}\\
 T_{\pkt}&=\exp(qW\ln(\lambda C_0)).\label{eq:Tpkt}
\end{align}
The packet speed is measured against the stretched throat infrastructure:
\begin{equation}
 \dot{X}_{\rm coord}=\frac{U}{B_{\vone}(X)}.
\label{eq:baware}
\end{equation}
This is a service-coordinate statement. The throat's radial stretch remains useful, and packet motion must account for it.

The passenger packet is the finite region
\begin{equation}
 |l-X(t)|\le r_{\pkt}.
\label{eq:packet_region}
\end{equation}
The moving packet norm is
\begin{equation}
 n_{\pkt}=-\alpha^2+\gamma_{ll}(\dot{X}_{\rm coord}+\beta^l)^2.
\label{eq:packet_norm}
\end{equation}
The stationary infrastructure monitor is
\begin{equation}
 g_{tt}=-\alpha^2+\gamma_{ll}(\beta^l)^2.
\label{eq:gtt}
\end{equation}
The distinction between Eq.~\eqref{eq:packet_norm} and Eq.~\eqref{eq:gtt} is a major conceptual result. The packet norm tracks the passenger worldtube. Stationary $g_{tt}$ tracks passive access and infrastructure state.

\section{Diagnostics: measuring the burden channels}

The reduced ADM diagnostic computes the extrinsic curvature
\begin{equation}
 K_{ij}=\frac{D_i\beta_j+D_j\beta_i-\partial_t\gamma_{ij}}{2\alpha},
\label{eq:Kij}
\end{equation}
the Hamiltonian source-demand proxy
\begin{equation}
 \rho_H=\frac{{}^{(3)}R+K^2-K_{ij}K^{ij}}{16\pi},
\label{eq:rhoH}
\end{equation}
and the momentum/current source-demand proxy
\begin{equation}
 j_M^i=\frac{D_j(K^{ij}-\gamma^{ij}K)}{8\pi}.
\label{eq:jM}
\end{equation}
The diagnostic groups these quantities into two channels:
\begin{align}
 J_{\dyn}&:\quad j_M\hbox{ and }K,\label{eq:Jdyn_words}\\
 J_{\geom}&:\quad \rho_H\hbox{ and }{}^{(3)}R.\label{eq:Jgeom_words}
\end{align}
The conservative source-exposure proxy is
\begin{equation}
 Tkk_{\rm floor}=\rho_H-2|j_M|.
\label{eq:tkk_floor}
\end{equation}
A pressure-sensitive bracket adds a pressure-scale term,
\begin{equation}
 Tkk_\eta=\rho_H+\eta\rho_H-2|j_M|,
\qquad \eta\in\{-1,0,+1\}.
\label{eq:tkk_eta}
\end{equation}
These screens are engineering diagnostics. They tell a source-construction program where the candidate asks for support.

\section{Result 1: passenger safety is packet-local}

The first composite result is a change in what is being protected. In a passive-tunnel reading, one tries to make the whole active throat feel like a corridor. In the packet reading, the passenger is associated with $n_{\pkt}<0$ along the finite moving worldtube. The infrastructure can carry time-dependent support and transition burden outside that worldtube.

This separation explains several otherwise confusing reduced-screen outcomes. A stationary observer at a support edge can see a different diagnostic from the moving packet. A low-lapse infrastructure state can trigger passive access monitors while the service packet remains timelike under correct choreography. The design target is therefore packet safety plus controlled infrastructure burden.

This result also clarifies how the combined geometry uses the wormhole v1 system. The hybrid throat supplies plant functions: radial stretch, flare support, shoulder matching, repayment/reset, and sampled-stress ledgers. The passenger rides the packet.

\section{Result 2: choreography is the hard packet gate}

The second result is operational. The packet is protected by the order of events. The service layer must catch the packet before the shift fades, and the throat support must relax after the packet has cleared the critical transition:
\begin{equation}
 x_{\rm catch}<x_\beta<x_q.
\label{eq:choreography_order}
\end{equation}
The tested thresholds show the structure. With $x_\beta=0.70$ and $x_q=1.25$, higher-speed/lower-margin cases require earlier catch. At $V=10$ and $\lambda=6$, catch that begins too late creates packet failure; edge shaping then operates on a packet that has already lost the support sequence it needed.

\begin{equation}

 \quad
 w_{\rm catch}=0.25,
 \quad w_\beta=0.28,

Early catch gives the packet time to slow before shift fade. Medium transition widths reduce gradients while keeping the service phases distinct. The $W^4$ shift taper cleans the support edge after the packet sequence is correct.

\section{Implications for source-supplied FTL engineering}

The work changes the engineering picture in five ways.

First, it makes the protected object explicit. The passenger rides a packet. The active throat supports the packet. That lets the design evaluate passenger safety where it belongs and evaluate plant burden where it belongs.

Second, it gives timing a mechanical role. Catch is the service operation that makes shift fade safe. The sequence catch, shift fade, throat relaxation is the control law of the packet service.

Third, it creates two source-demand channels. Transition profiles move $j_M$ and $K$; capacity/support geometry moves $\rho_H$ and ${}^{(3)}R$. This is the map a source-construction program can use. It says which source component should be designed to carry current, and which source component should be designed to carry spatial curvature.

Fourth, it identifies angular capacity as real infrastructure. A purely radial service geometry can give low current and high curvature burden. Moderate angular capacity reduces the curvature burden. This is the strongest capacity-design lesson in the paper.

Fifth, it shows alignment between the geometry screen and the source-exposure screen. The frozen candidate reduces the aggregate source-demand proxy and also reduces sampled packet current-floor exposure. The packet channel improves more strongly than the total score alone suggests.
